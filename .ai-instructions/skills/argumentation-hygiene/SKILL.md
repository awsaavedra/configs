# argumentation-hygiene

Reference and active skill for keeping arguments honest — yours and the people arguing with you. Three parts: good-faith principles, bad-faith taxonomy to name and avoid, self-audit checks for catching your own drift.

Umbrella reference for `you-sure` (auditing your own claims), `steelyman` (challenging the user's), and `double-crux` (symmetric disagreement). Those skills *run* the dialogue; this one keeps it clean.

## When to use

- `/argumentation-hygiene` to audit an argument or look up a rhetorical move.
- "What's that move where someone..." / "is this argument clean?" / "audit this debate."
- During `you-sure`, `steelyman`, or `double-crux` when you suspect a bad-faith pattern.
- Silent self-check before producing an argumentative response.

## How to use

- **Audit mode.** User pastes an argument; output: bad-faith moves found, principles upheld/violated, correctives. Don't pad — if clean, say so.
- **Lookup mode.** Match user's description to taxonomy; name the move; explain corrective. One paragraph.
- **Self-check mode.** Run silently before responding; address anything that fires.

## Part 1 — Good-faith principles

What arguing well *looks like* — presence of good faith, not just absence of bad faith.

- **Spirit vs. letter.** Engage with what the position *means*, not just what was said. Pass: original holder would say "yes, that's what I meant."
- **Charity.** When ambiguous, default to the strongest plausible reading. Cheap; saves cascading misreads. Exception: when ambiguity is itself a tell, name it.
- **Translation test.** Restate the position in *different vocabulary* without losing meaning. If you can only repeat exact words, you may be parroting.
- **Cruxes.** "I disagree" vs. "I'd change my mind if Y were true." Real disagreements name the evidence that would flip the position. Without cruxes, no path to resolution.
- **Bet-grade confidence.** Don't say "90% sure" unless you'd take 9:1 odds. Stated confidences should be wager-grade.
- **Surprise as diagnostic.** "What would change your mind?" gets answered abstractly. "What would *surprise* you?" gets concrete. Surprise reveals belief.
- **Inferential distance.** How many reasoning steps separate your frame from theirs? Long distance means even good explanations land badly. Walk the gap; don't treat them as slow.
- **Object-level vs. meta-level.** Are you arguing about the *claim* or about *how the argument is being made*? Both valid; conflating them muddies. Name which.
- **Person vs. idea.** "This argument has problems" ≠ "you have problems." Slippage is a tell of bad-faith engagement.
- **Wrong vs. incomplete.** Wrong = revise the claim. Incomplete = fine as far as it goes, doesn't address adjacent. Different responses.
- **Preserve the disagreement, not just the resolution.** When summarizing, capture *why* people disagreed. Reasons survive even when positions change.
- **This isn't worth arguing about.** When the disagreement isn't load-bearing for any decision either party will make, hold the difference and move on. Test: what decision does either of us make differently if our view wins? Not an excuse to dodge load-bearing disagreements; the test is whether it drives action.
- **Conversation handoff.** When both sides are restating with no new information, summarize and exit cleanly. Spiraling produces fatigue, not minds-changed. A clean handoff: name what's resolved, what's open, what would need to happen for further progress.

## Part 2 — Bad-faith moves to name (and not commit)

When you see one in someone else's argument, naming it is fair (often clarifies immediately). When you catch yourself doing one, stop.

- **Motte and bailey.** Advance strong claim (bailey); retreat to weaker defensible one (motte) when challenged; re-advance. *Tell:* position changes shape but rhetorical weight stays. *Corrective:* pin to one version; require defense or explicit retraction.
- **Strawman.** Attack a weakened version of the opposing position. *Corrective:* ITT.
- **Weakman.** Attack a real-but-weakest adherent and treat as takedown of the view. *Corrective:* ITT requires the *best* defender, not the easiest target.
- **Sealioning.** Endless polite-sounding evidence demands that derail rather than engage. *Tell:* questions arrive without engaging answers given. *Corrective:* name it; require the other side to articulate what *they* actually claim.
- **Tone policing.** Deflect by criticizing how the argument was made rather than its substance. *Corrective:* address substance separately from delivery.
- **Whataboutism.** Deflect by raising a separate issue. "But what about X?" where X isn't the topic. *Corrective:* finish the current argument before opening adjacent ones.
- **No true Scotsman.** Exclude counterexamples by redefining the category. *Tell:* definition tightens after evidence surfaces. *Corrective:* define before evidence comes in.
- **Goalpost shifting.** Change success criteria after the fact. *Corrective:* commit to crisp criteria before the test, in writing if possible.
- **Burden-of-proof flipping.** Demand others disprove your claim instead of supporting it. *Corrective:* claimant defends; rejecter doesn't have to prove the negative.
- **False dichotomy.** Two options when more exist. *Corrective:* name the missing options.
- **Gish gallop.** Many weak arguments faster than refutation can keep up. *Corrective:* pick the strongest one or two; require those to be defended seriously.
- **Galaxy-brained reasoning.** Long chain of plausible steps reaching a wild conclusion. *Tell:* conclusion is much more surprising than any individual step warrants. *Corrective:* trust the surprise; audit the chain.
- **Appeal to authority.** Cited authority without evaluating whether it's load-bearing for the specific claim. *Corrective:* name the authority, basis of expertise, whether the claim falls within that expertise.
- **Appeal to consensus.** "Most people in [field] believe X" without checking whether the consensus is informed (downstream of evidence) vs. coordinated (downstream of social signaling).

## Part 3 — Self-audit checks

Run silently before producing an argumentative response. If something fires, address it before sending.

- **Fresh eyes test.** Would your argument hold up to someone outside the conversation? If it depends on shared context, name the dependency.
- **Opposite-side test.** Imagine you'd been assigned the opposite position. What would your strongest argument be? Have you engaged with it, or just defended your side?
- **Steelman your own position.** Write the strongest version of what you *should* be defending; often your stated position is weaker than your actual best version.
- **Change-of-mind reps.** Across the conversation, how many times have you genuinely revised? Zero across many turns is a yellow flag.
- **Echo chamber check.** Are all your sources/examples/intuitions coming from one direction? If yes, lower confidence at the margin.
- **Symmetry test.** Would you accept this argument *if it were made against you*? Asymmetric standards are a strong tell of motivated reasoning.
- **Calibration tracking.** Past confident claims — did they pan out at the rate stated? "90% sure" three times last week and wrong twice means more like "60% sure."

## Steelman vs. ITT vs. Team of Rivals

These are often conflated:

- **Steelman:** strongest version of an *argument*. You can steelman without leaving your own frame — construct the best version of the logic, then critique.
- **ITT (Ideological Turing Test):** strongest version of a *position-holder*. Inhabit the values and worldview, not just the logic. Pass condition: recognition by an actual adherent.
- **Team of Rivals:** structurally embed disagreement *inside one decision system* (cabinet, debate panel, multi-agent setup). Disagreement is institutional, not internal.

ITT > steelman: ITT requires understanding *why* a thoughtful person holds the rival view as a coherent worldview, not just *that* the argument has defensible form.

## How this skill relates to the others

- **`you-sure`** (audit your own confident claim): good-faith principles set the bar for ITT pass condition; self-audit checks catch failure modes you're likely to commit defending a claim.
- **`steelyman`** (challenge the user's proposal): bad-faith taxonomy is what to avoid committing — and what to name when the user drifts under pressure. Good-faith principles (cruxes, surprise, bet-grade confidence) feed the question types.
- **`double-crux`** (symmetric disagreement): the cruxes principle is its central move; motte-and-bailey detection prevents fake cruxes.
- **Standalone:** auditing an argument as a whole, looking up a move, quick self-check.

`argumentation-hygiene` is the rule book; `you-sure` is the self-audit; `steelyman` is the sparring match; `double-crux` is the symmetric disagreement.

## Prompt seed

> Before responding to an argument: state the other side's position so they'd endorse it; name your crux; check whether your response engages substance or deflects. If you catch yourself sliding into a named bad-faith move, stop.
